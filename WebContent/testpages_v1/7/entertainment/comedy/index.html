Comedy

Mr. Fator (pronounced FAY-tor) was a showbiz journeyman until he won the second season of the NBC competition show “America’s Got Talent” in 2007. Since then, he has become an amazing success story. He is a permanent presence at the Mirage resort in Las Vegas, and Forbes recently ranked him No. 2 on a list of top-earning comics in the country, after Jerry Seinfeld, estimating his earnings over the 12-month period that ended June 1 at $24 million. What’s the attraction? First, he has a singular collection of skills. He’s an excellent ventriloquist, effortless where others somehow draw attention to those lips that aren’t moving. He’s also a pretty good impersonator. And though his singing voice isn’t strong enough to support a career as a crooner alone, he’s better than whoever sang at the last wedding you attended. But plenty of reality-show phenoms — most of them, actually — have had talent yet disappeared after their small-screen triumphs. One reason Mr. Fator became an exception was that he was already a seasoned performer when he went on “America’s Got Talent.” As he told the Newark audience, he had spent years working on whatever stage would have him, both as a ventriloquist and as a would-be rocker. In one amusing bit on Friday, he turned his impersonation skills on himself in meta fashion, doing impressions of a young Terry Fator trying to impersonate rock stars. So when fame finally found him in his 40s, Mr. Fator was already comfortable onstage and knew how to work an audience, skills he was able to translate to the much bigger arenas he could suddenly fill. But just as important is that he seems to know who will be most impressed by his abilities and tailors his material to that particular crowd.

The audience in Newark was very much what you see in the average casino: middle age to graying (and, for many of the men, balding); dressed down rather than dressed up. It was also overwhelmingly white. And Mr. Fator and his seven-piece band gave the crowd what it no doubt wanted: mild jokes that made reference to football and ancient television shows; snippets of oldies (“Duke of Earl,” “Earth Angel”); a support-the-troops segment that included asking military personnel to stand and be applauded. Another ventriloquist, Jeff Dunham, is No. 4 on that Forbes list and got there with eyebrow-raising characters like Achmed the Dead Terrorist. Mr. Fator isn’t much interested in shock value. He favors gentle alter egos like Maynard, an Elvis impersonator; Monty Carlo, a lounge singer; and Winston the Impersonating Turtle. And the humor that came out of these puppets and dummies, and out of Mr. Fator himself, on Friday was decidedly nonthreatening. Compared with the material many stand-up comics work today — rape jokes, bits peppered with racial epithets, graphic sex stories — his Newark set was PG with an occasional nod to PG-13. It didn’t get much more scandalous than the line uttered by Duggie, a stoner puppet. “When life gives you lemons,” Duggie advised, “roll ’em up and smoke ’em.”
